## Abstract

As a heterogeneous disease, endometriosis is associated with diagnostic delay. Delayed diagnosis, physical discomfort, hormone therapy, and inconvenience in daily life and work all contribute to a decreased quality of life for endometriosis patients. Early clinical diagnosis is highly important for the intervention and treatment of endometriosis. Currently, reliable non-invasive diagnostic methods are lacking, and laparoscopic examination combined with pathological diagnosis is considered the "gold standard" for definitively diagnosing endometriosis. An increasing number of studies have confirmed the correlation between endometriosis and microbial ecological changes. Microbial dysbiosis is an important factor in the development and progression of endometriosis. Certain key microbial species and their metabolites can induce functional alterations in endometrial cells through various mechanisms, often preceding the emergence of clinical symptoms. Endometriosis are chronic inflammatory diseases, with an immunoinflammatory response as the pathological foundation. The microbiome may participate in the pathological mechanisms of endometriosis through multiple pathways, including mediating inflammatory responses, regulating immune responses, participating in estrogen regulation, interfering with metabolic activities, and modulating the gut–brain axis. Therefore, the microbiome holds potential as an early non-invasive diagnostic and therapeutic target for endometriosis patients.

## Key Findings

### Microbial Dysbiosis in Endometriosis

| Site | Key Changes |
|------|------------|
| **Gut** | ↑ Firmicutes/Bacteroidetes ratio, ↑ *Shigella/E. coli*, ↓ Clostridia, ↑ *Ruminococcus* |
| **Vagina** | ↓ *Lactobacillus*, ↑ *Gardnerella*, ↑ *Atopobium* (Actinobacteria) |
| **Cervix** | ↓ richness/diversity, ↑ Enterobacteriaceae, ↑ *Streptococcus* |
| **Uterus** | ↓ Lactobacillaceae, ↑ Streptomycetaceae, Staphylococcaceae, Enterobacteriaceae; hallmark: *Pseudomonas*, *Acinetobacter* |
| **Peritoneal fluid** | ↑ *Acinetobacter*, *Pseudomonas*, *Streptococcus*, *Alishewanella*, *Enterococcus*; ↓ *Lactobacillus* |
| **Oral** | ↑ *Streptococcus*, *Haemophilus parasuis*, *Prevotella* |

### Mechanisms Linking Microbiota to Endometriosis

1. **Inflammatory response:** LPS from Gram-negative bacteria → TLR4 activation → pro-inflammatory cytokines → pelvic inflammatory milieu → angiogenesis and lesion growth
2. **Immune dysregulation:** Microbiota → impaired NK cells, T-cell exhaustion via PD-1/PD-L1 pathway → reduced immune clearance of ectopic cells
3. **Estrogen metabolism (estrobolome):** Gut bacteria produce β-glucuronidase → deconjugation of estrogens → increased active estrogen → hyperestrogenic state → lesion growth
4. **Lipid metabolism:** Dysbiosis → altered bile acids, ↓ short-chain fatty acids (butyrate), ↓ α-linolenic acid → impaired intestinal barrier → inflammation
5. **Brain-gut axis:** Microbiota → neuroactive metabolites (glutamate, GABA) → H-P-O axis → estrogen secretion → pain modulation

### Clinical Applications

- **Non-invasive biomarkers:** *Anaerococcus* (vaginal) for staging; *Ruminococcus* (gut) + *Pseudomonas* (pelvic) for early diagnosis; vaginal microbiota + CA125 for pain discrimination
- **Therapeutic approaches:**
  - Dietary intervention (probiotics, prebiotics, fiber)
  - Probiotic supplementation (*Lactobacillus* strains)
  - Fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT)
  - Antibiotics (targeting pathogenic bacteria)
  - Postbiotics (butyrate supplementation)

---

## Conclusion

Microbial dysbiosis plays a pivotal role in the initiation and progression of endometriosis through multiple pathways — inflammatory, immune, estrogenic, metabolic, and neurological. The microbiome represents a promising avenue for early non-invasive diagnosis and targeted therapeutic intervention.
